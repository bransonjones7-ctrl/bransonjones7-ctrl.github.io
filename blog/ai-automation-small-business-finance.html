What Building Automations Taught Me About Small Business Finance

Branson Jones · July 2026 · 4 min read

My job is financial reporting and reconciliations for a firm in Monroeville. On my own time, I build automated systems that handle the same category of work.

Doing both teaches you something uncomfortable. A lot of what small businesses pay for in finance is not analysis. It is transportation: moving numbers from one system into another.

The work nobody should be billing hours for

Reconciliations are the clearest example. Matching a credit card statement to a general ledger is essential work, but it is pattern matching. Software has been better than people at pattern matching for a while now.

The same is true of monthly reporting packets and the manual formatting work behind most leadership meetings.

The economics of automating this have shifted fast. Coverage of enterprise automation platforms shows adoption accelerating through 2026, and the same underlying tools now cost a small business almost nothing to use.

Where people still earn their keep

Here is the part that keeps me optimistic about a finance career. Automation moves numbers, but it does not decide what they mean, and it does not sit in the room when a hard call gets made.

Judgment and trust are still human work. What changes is the ratio. The analyst of the next decade spends less time assembling reports and more time defending a recommendation.

It also reframes the market. An owner weighing roughly $2,000 a month for an automated reporting system against $70,000 or more a year for a junior analyst is not weighing much. The durable career sits on the oversight side of that trade, and I intend to be there.

If you run a small business, the question is no longer whether this technology is ready. It is which of your recurring finance tasks you would never assign to a person again if you saw the alternative once.
