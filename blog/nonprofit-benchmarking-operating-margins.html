What Benchmarking Fourteen Nonprofits Taught Me About Operating Margins

Branson Jones · July 2026 · 4 min read

This year I built full financial statements for a fourteen-organization peer group of human services nonprofits, working from their public Form 990 filings. Full statements, from the income statement down to cash flow, then a ratio scorecard comparing them all.

Anyone can do a version of this. Every 990 is public through ProPublica's Nonprofit Explorer, and it is one of the best free educations in real-world finance available.

Margins are thinner than you think

The headline lesson: organizations serving the same population under the same funding programs can have wildly different financial health.

Some peers in the study ran deficits, spending more than a dollar for every dollar of revenue. Others posted healthy surpluses at many times the scale. Same industry, opposite trajectories.

The ratios that actually separate them

Three things explained most of the gap. First, revenue model: waiver billing and grant funding behave completely differently when costs rise. Second, personnel cost ratio, since wages dominate every budget in direct care.

Third, and least appreciated: occupancy and asset efficiency. Organizations carrying property that is not yet generating service revenue look weaker on paper even when the strategy behind it is sound.

A ratio next to thirteen peers is a story.
